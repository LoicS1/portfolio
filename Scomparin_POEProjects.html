Entrepreneur Project
May 2017, Principles of Engineering Design

The goal of this four-person project was to design a solution to any real-world problem. My team decided to build a physical prototype, using VEX parts, of a machine that would remove unwanted sediment in reservoirs. We had to use lightweight yet strong material, such as steel and titanium for the frame, while designing a net system to pick up sediment, which was the most cost efficient system available.

Our final solution consisted of a submarine with a rigid, metal exterior frame and central cavity, equipped at the front with wire mesh and light sensor at the front opening, and a fine net and metal plates on the rear opening. The submarine would patrol the reservoir using its mechanical propellers, and begin filtering sediment in the back net when the light sensor detected the water was murky and full of suspended sediment.

As project leader, I was responsible for managing most of the project documentation, part of the presentation, communicating with my teammates regarding the project design and timeline, and designing the submarine's exterior frame. I learned a lot in my field of interest, environmental engineering, by analyzing how different designs would respond to a complex environmental problem (sediment accumulation). However, this project also introduced me to a multi-disciplinary problem-solving approach, as I was also exposed to the programming of the sensor and testing of the submarine's mechanical structure.

Project 3.2.4 Machine Control Design
April 2017, Principles of Engineering Design

The objective for this project was to build a machine satisfying an unmet human need, while using VEX robotics hardware, including sensors, as well as the RobotC programming language. My group was asked to find several different winch, pulley, rack and pinion, and drum systems that would satisfy the design requirement for our machine. We built an elevator that utilized a sensor and button system to pick up people on different floors quickly, as well as come down to the ground floor immediately in case of an emergency such as an earthquake, or when not used for a certain period of time.

We used the rack and pinion system as it provided the greatest structural stability for the design; with a gear attached to one side of the elevator, the other side was supported by a steel slider. Additionally, we used an ultrasonic sensor to measure the height of the elevator to make it stop at the correct floor and bump switches to call the elevator to each floor. During the project, I was responsible for the physical design of the elevator using VEX parts, including spacing out the sensors correctly to match input values used in the RobotC program, allowing me to work directly with the programmer and the other members of the team working on documenting the project updates. The biggest issue was getting the elevator to move up and down smoothly along the slider- often, the gear would get stuck in the rack because of too much frictional force. This issue was solved by rebuilding the elevator to a new size and using more flexible metal parts.

This project was eye-opening because I was able to see how mechanics, electronics, and programming were combined into one cohesive design. I now have a better idea of how to build "smart" systems using sensors and could apply this to both the mechanical and environmental fields by designing efficient home energy systems that react to differences in temperature and light.
